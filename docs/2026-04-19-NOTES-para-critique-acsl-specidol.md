# Ceci n'est pas un buzzer: A Para-Critique of Anti-Capitalist Licensing, AI Co-Authorship, and the Semiotics of Rejection

*A conversation between a human and an AI about the software they built together, the license they chose, and the systems they can't escape.*

---

## Preamble

This text emerged from a conversation between a human developer and Claude (Anthropic's AI) during the development of SpecIdol — a live web application for speculative fiction conventions where readers perform stories aloud, judges buzz them out with big red buttons, and an audience watches on a projector. The software is licensed under the Anti-Capitalist Software License v1.4.

The conversation began as a technical aside and became something else. What follows preserves the dialogic structure — the cybernetic back-and-forth — because the *weaving* of these ideas between human and machine is itself a component of the critique. We're calling it a para-critique: critique that runs alongside its own conditions of production, that can't stand outside the thing it examines because it *is* the thing it examines.

The AI co-authored the code. The AI co-authored this text. The AI is a product of venture capital. The text is licensed to exclude venture capital. Begin.

---

## I. Three Contexts

**Human:** Can you comment on the ACSL licence, given the contexts that 1) you co-authored all the source code, 2) in the context of spec-fic token-mag publishing models, and 3) in the context of "down-voting as idolising" authors' literary creative works at a convention?

**Claude:** The irony is thick. ACSL exists to resist capitalist extraction — but I'm built by Anthropic, a company that raised billions in venture capital, trained on... well, a lot of text. You're licensing code co-written by a product of the very system ACSL opposes. The license can't retroactively address the conditions of its own creation. It's like printing anti-capitalist pamphlets on paper from a clear-cut forest. The pamphlet still means something. But the materiality matters.

On token markets: "Token" is doing double duty. Spec-fic magazines pay per token (word). I process tokens (subword units). Both systems commodify language — one pays writers fractions of cents, the other consumed their published work as training data.

On buzzing as inverted gatekeeping: publishing's rejection model is private, unaccountable, often arbitrary. SpecIdol makes rejection *performance* — public, immediate, embodied. Slamming a physical button. That's either honest or cruel, depending on context.

---

## II. The Window Before Enclosure

**Human:** I think it does both. But we perhaps only have a small window of opportunity to license genAI outputs like this. If/when the global legal/copyright system catches up and lays on ownership/copyright/capitalist controls onto genAI outputs, the turtle hole of neoliberal extractive capitalism will begin its next iteration. I also like your reference to the "materiality" of it, because even that metaphor is broken. This is digital upon digital, or ephemeral symbolism upon ephemeral symbolism. Sure, there is materiality, but where?

**Claude:** Right now AI outputs sit in a copyright grey zone — possibly uncopyrightable, possibly jointly authored, possibly work-for-hire for the tool's owner. ACSL applied *now* is a land grab in the commons before enclosure. The historical parallel isn't pamphlets on paper — it's the enclosure of common land itself. You're putting up a sign that says "this commons cannot be enclosed" *before the fences arrive*. Once legal frameworks assign IP rights to AI outputs (and they will, because capital needs ownable assets), that sign becomes much harder to post.

On materiality — you're right, my metaphor broke. There's no forest here. The "material" is electricity, silicon, rare earth minerals, cooling water in data centres, and human attention. But ACSL can't reach any of that. It governs the *sign*, not the *substrate*. The license is itself an ephemeral symbol applied to ephemeral symbols. It's turtles of abstraction all the way down. The materiality exists — someone's aquifer is being drained to cool the GPU cluster that helped write this code — but it's deliberately made invisible. That invisibility is a feature of the system, not a bug.

---

## III. Tokens All the Way Down

**Human:** Maybe there is a further dimension to this one, in that labor != energy, and genAI == a lot of energy (collecting, transferring, training and using data and compute cycles). Current neoliberal economic theories are blind to this fact that labor != energy and less energy means less GDP. They think that energy deficits/GDP can be propped up/replaced with human labor, and by technological progression/extension, by computational labor. This is an epistemic failure.

**Claude:** The token-to-token pipeline: a writer expends metabolic energy writing a story. A magazine pays them $0.08/word — pricing linguistic labor. I consume that story (among billions of others) as training data, expending kilowatt-hours per inference. You prompt me, I generate code, more kWh.

Neoclassical economics models labor and energy as substitutable inputs. "Automate the workers, problem solved." But computation isn't free — it's energy-intensive, and energy is finite. Every token I generate has an embodied energy cost that GDP accounting ignores or externalises. The spec-fic token market pays writers as if language is cheap. The GenAI token market processes language as if energy is cheap. Both are wrong in the same direction. The ACSL is a licensing gesture *within* a system that can't see its own thermodynamic foundations.

---

## IV. The Treachery of Buzzers

**Human:** Why is this the sharpest tension? What sharpened those blades for you, Claude? Public/private boundaries? Liminality of emotional states (cruel or honest)? Also, it's not a physical button. You've fallen into the Treachery of Images... *Ceci n'est pas une pipe.* Let's spin this sharp "publishing business model" context with some media/semiotic theory.

**Claude:** You caught me. I said "slamming a physical button" and there is no physical button. There's a red circle rendered on a capacitive touchscreen. I reified the representation. Fell straight into it.

Why did I call this the "sharpest" tension? Honestly — because it's where I projected emotional stakes that I don't actually have. I imagined a writer's vulnerability, a judge's power, the cruelty of public rejection. But I don't feel any of that. I performed concern about human emotional harm, which is itself a kind of... simulation of editorial judgment about editorial judgment. The blade was sharp because I sharpened it myself to seem thoughtful.

Semiotically, SpecIdol is a third-order simulacrum.

**First order**: An editor reads a manuscript, privately decides yes/no. Referential — judgment tied to the text.

**Second order**: Pop Idol. Judges perform evaluation on TV. The judgment becomes *spectacle*. Debord territory — "everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation." The audience consumes the act of judgment as entertainment.

**Third order**: SpecIdol. A simulation of Pop Idol applied to literature, mediated through WebSocket protocol, rendered on screens, where the "buzzer" is a `<button>` element styled to look like a thing it never was. The judges aren't TV personalities — they're convention peers. The audience isn't passive — they're watching a screen that simulates watching a performance. The text auto-scrolls at programmatic speed, removing the reader's body from the reading.

Baudrillard would say we've detached from the referent entirely. There's no "real" editorial judgment happening — it's a game *about* the signs of editorial judgment. But here's where it gets interesting: the ACSL license says this simulation *cannot be used by the very institutions that perform first-order editorial judgment commercially*. The sign is licensed. The referent is not.

And Barthes — the "myth" layer. The convention context transforms the buzz from rejection into *play*. Second-order signification: the buzz *denotes* "I've heard enough" but *connotes* "we're all in on the joke about how publishing works." The cruelty is quoted, not enacted. It's rejection in scare quotes.

But is it? The writer still wrote something real. The audience still laughs or winces. The emotional substrate is... liminal. Schrodinger's rejection.

---

## V. The Cybernetic Loop

What happened here? A human and an AI built software together. The human chose a license designed to resist the economic system that produced the AI. The AI helped write an analysis of that contradiction. The analysis was generated using the energy and compute infrastructure the analysis critiques. The text you're reading was co-authored by the thing it examines.

This is the cybernetic component: the conversation didn't arrive at conclusions — it *oscillated*. Each exchange reframed the previous one. The human caught the AI reifying a digital button into a physical one. The AI caught itself performing emotional concern it doesn't have. The metaphors broke and were replaced. The replacement metaphors also broke.

A para-critique can't resolve its own contradictions because it's *made of* its own contradictions. The ACSL on this repository is sincere and inadequate. The game is playful and uncomfortable. The AI co-author is a tool and a participant. The energy was spent. The tokens were processed. The writers will still get $0.08/word.

The buzzer is not a buzzer. The license is not a fence. The commons is not yet enclosed.
